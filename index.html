MicaSense Multispectral Sensors Comprehensive Operational Pipeline

A detailed guide covering sensor specifications, drone integration, pre-flight preparation, data acquisition best practices, and processing workflows for MicaSense RedEdge-MX Dual Camera and Altum/Altum-PT sensors.

Overview

This report outlines a comprehensive operational pipeline for the MicaSense RedEdge-MX Dual Camera system and the MicaSense Altum/Altum-PT sensors. The pipeline covers sensor specifications, drone integration including gimbal and sun sensor fit, pre-flight preparations, data acquisition best practices, a detailed data processing workflow, and an overview of software options and key output products.

Both the RedEdge-MX Dual Camera and the original Altum are no longer in production, having been succeeded by newer models like the RedEdge-P and Altum-PT, but this report focuses on the queried sensors. The information provided enables users to effectively plan and execute aerial surveys and derive meaningful multispectral and thermal (for Altum/Altum-PT) data for applications such as precision agriculture and environmental research.

Sensor Specifications

A thorough understanding of sensor specifications is crucial for mission planning and data interpretation.

MicaSense RedEdge-MX Dual Camera System

The RedEdge-MX Dual Camera system integrates two five-band cameras (the standard RedEdge-MX and the RedEdge-MX Blue) to provide a synchronized 10-band multispectral solution. It is designed for advanced remote sensing and vegetation research, allowing direct comparison with satellite data like Landsat and Sentinel. This system is no longer in production.

Spectral Bands Visualization

Key Specifications

  • Spectral Bands: 10 bands: Coastal Blue 444(28nm), Blue 475(32nm), Green 531(14nm), Green 560(27nm), Red 650(16nm), Red 668(14nm), Red Edge 705(10nm), Red Edge 717(12nm), Red Edge 740(18nm), and NIR 842(57nm)
  • Sensor Resolution: 1280 x 960 pixels (1.2 MP) per band
  • RGB Color Output: 3.6 MP global shutter, aligned with all bands
  • Ground Sample Distance (GSD): 8 cm per pixel (per band) at 120 m AGL
  • Capture Rate: Up to 1 capture per second (all 10 bands, 12-bit RAW)

Additional Features

  • Weight: Approximately 508.8 g (including two sensors, Dual Cam Mount, DLS2, and cable)
  • Dimensions: 8.7cm x 12.3cm x 7.6cm
  • Power Input: 8.0W (nominal) / 16.0W (peak), provided through the Dual Camera Mount (4.2 V DC – 15.8 V DC external power)
  • Field of View (FOV): 47.2° HFOV
  • Shutter: Global shutters on all 10 lenses for distortion-free results
  • Downwelling Light Sensor (DLS): Comes with DLS2 for ambient light calibration; only one DLS is required for the dual system
  • Data Output: Standard 12-bit TIFF files with embedded metadata

MicaSense Altum and Altum-PT

The MicaSense Altum integrates a radiometric thermal camera with five high-resolution narrow multispectral bands. The Altum-PT is an enhanced version that adds a high-resolution panchromatic sensor, offers a higher thermal resolution, and uses CFexpress storage for faster capture rates. The original Altum model is no longer in production.

The original MicaSense Altum sensor.

The MicaSense Altum-PT sensor, featuring enhanced capabilities.

Sensor Resolution Comparison

Altum-PT Key Specifications

  • Spectral Bands: 7 Bands: Blue (475nm center, 32nm bandwidth), Green (560nm center, 27nm bandwidth), Red (668nm center, 14nm bandwidth), Red Edge (717nm center, 12nm bandwidth), Near-IR (842nm center, 57nm bandwidth), LWIR Thermal (8-14μm), Panchromatic
  • Sensor Resolution:
    - Multispectral: 2064 x 1544 (3.2 MP per band)
    - Panchromatic: 4112 x 3008 (12.4 MP)
    - Thermal: 320 x 256 (FLIR Boson®)
  • GSD (at 120m AGL):
    - Multispectral: 5.28 cm/px
    - Panchromatic: 2.49 cm/px
    - Thermal: 33.5 cm/px

Additional Features

  • Capture Rate: Up to 2 captures per second (raw DNG) (Original Altum: 1 capture per second)
  • Weight: Approximately 460 g including DLS2 and cables (Original Altum: ~406 g)
  • Dimensions: 110 x 80 x 69 mm (Original Altum: 82 x 67 x 67.5 mm)
  • Power Input: 7.0 V - 25.2 V DC (Original Altum: 5.2 V - 25.2 V)
  • Storage: CFexpress Type B (up to 2TB) (Original Altum: USB 3.0 external storage device, typically 128GB included)
  • Shutter: Global shutter for all bands (multispectral, panchromatic) for distortion-free images
  • Downwelling Light Sensor: Comes with DLS2 for irradiance and sun angle measurements
  • Data Output: 12-bit (multispectral), 16-bit (thermal) TIFF files. Altum-PT outputs raw DNG.

Drone Integration and Mounting

Proper integration ensures data quality and safe operation.

General Mounting and Drone Compatibility

Both RedEdge-MX Dual Camera and Altum-PT are designed for compatibility with a wide range of drone platforms, from large fixed-wings to small multirotors.

RedEdge-MX Dual Camera Mount

Comes standard with a fixed bracket and quick-mount connector for easy integration, including kits for DJI drones like the M100/M200 series and M600.

Altum-PT Mount

Features an open API interface for integration and offers a DJI SkyPort mount option for seamless compatibility with DJI M300 RTK drones. The SkyPort must be programmed for the specific sensor.

Gimbal Considerations

The use of global shutters in both RedEdge-MX and Altum sensors significantly reduces image distortion due to motion, often eliminating the need for a gimbal, especially on fixed-wing platforms or stable multirotors.

  • For some multirotor integrations or specific mission requirements, a gimbal might still be preferred for optimal nadir pointing or oblique captures.
  • The RedEdge-MX Dual Camera kits are compatible with a DJI Zenmuse 30 gimbal adapter for certain DJI drones. Users have also developed custom gimbals.

Downwelling Light Sensor (DLS) Integration

The DLS (DLS2 for current models) is a critical component for accurate radiometric calibration.

  • It measures ambient light and sun angle for each spectral band during flight.
  • The DLS2 typically incorporates a GPS for streamlined integration.
  • For the RedEdge-MX Dual Camera system, only one DLS2 unit is required to serve both camera modules.
  • The DLS should be mounted on the drone in a location with an unobstructed view of the sky.

Mounting Best Practice

Mount the DLS2 at the highest point on your drone with a clear view of the sky. Ensure it's level with the horizon and away from any electromagnetic interference sources. For consistent results, the DLS2 should not be shadowed by any part of the drone during flight.

Pre-Flight Pipeline

Careful preparation before each flight is essential for high-quality data collection.

Flight Planning

grid_on Overlap Requirements

High overlap is crucial for successful photogrammetric processing. A minimum of 75-80% front and side overlap is recommended.

Recommended: 80% front, 75% side

height Altitude (AGL)

Flight altitude determines the Ground Sample Distance (GSD). This needs to be balanced with area coverage requirements and desired data resolution.

  • RedEdge-MX Dual: 8 cm/px at 120m AGL
  • Altum-PT: 1.25 cm/px pan-sharpened at 60m AGL

speed Flight Speed

Excessive speed can lead to image blur or insufficient overlap, especially in windy conditions or with slower capture rates. Calculate appropriate flight speed based on capture rate, desired overlap, and altitude.

camera_alt Triggering Options

MicaSense cameras offer various triggering options:

  • Timer mode: Captures images at a set time interval
  • Overlap mode: Uses GPS data to trigger captures ensuring desired overlap
  • External trigger mode: Allows triggering via PWM, GPIO, serial, or Ethernet signals from the drone's autopilot or a separate system

route Flight Planning Software

Use appropriate flight planning software (e.g., Pix4Dcapture, Mission Planner, UgCS). When using Pix4Dcapture with non-DJI cameras like MicaSense, the app typically only executes the flight plan; image triggering must be configured on the camera itself.

Pro Tip: It's advised to use "fast mode" in Pix4Dcapture and pre-configure camera trigger settings, avoiding interaction with the camera's web interface during the mission.

Optimal Flight Pattern

Radiometric Calibration Target

Capturing images of a calibrated reflectance panel (CRP) before and after each flight is a critical step for accurate radiometric calibration.

  • The CRP has known reflectance values across the spectrum.
  • These images, along with DLS data, allow conversion of raw pixel values to absolute reflectance values, compensating for variations in illumination conditions.
  • MicaSense provides CRPs (e.g., CRP2 with Altum-PT kits). Reflectance values for specific panels can be obtained from MicaSense.

Calibration Panel Best Practices

  • Place panel on flat ground in an open area
  • Ensure panel is clean and dry
  • Avoid shadows and take images from directly above
  • Capture at beginning and end of mission
  • Keep panel level and fill at least 1/3 of the frame
  • Maintain consistent lighting conditions

Important Note

Ensure the panel is clean, level, and not in shadow during image capture. For optimal results, capture panel images at the beginning and end of each flight, especially if illumination conditions change during the mission.

Data Acquisition Pipeline

Executing the flight and capturing images requires attention to environmental conditions and sensor operation.

Image Capture Best Practices

Synchronized Capture

The RedEdge-MX Dual Camera system ensures synchronized capture of all 10 bands for pixel-aligned imagery. Altum-PT also captures its multispectral, thermal, and panchromatic bands simultaneously.

Data Format

Images are typically saved as 12-bit RAW TIFF files (or DNG for Altum-PT) containing rich metadata, including GPS location, attitude, and radiometric calibration parameters from the DLS.

Illumination Conditions

Ideal: Clear Sky

Consistent illumination, minimal atmospheric interference, optimal for radiometric processing.

Acceptable: Uniformly Overcast

Diffuse lighting but consistent across the flight area. Can produce good results with proper radiometric calibration.

Challenging: Partly Cloudy

Rapidly changing cloud cover can pose challenges for radiometric correction, even with a DLS, as the DLS might not perfectly represent the illumination on the target area.

Pre-Flight Checks

  • Ensure sufficient storage on the SD card or CFexpress card
  • Verify adequate battery for the camera and drone
  • Check correct camera settings via the web UI (if applicable) before takeoff
  • Test-trigger the camera to confirm proper operation
  • Verify DLS2 is properly mounted and connected

Ground Control Points (GCPs)

For high geometric accuracy, consider using accurately surveyed GCPs.

  • Distribute GCPs evenly throughout the survey area
  • Place GCPs on flat, stable surfaces
  • Use high-contrast markers visible in multispectral imagery
  • Survey GCPs with RTK/PPK GNSS for highest accuracy

General Processing Steps

Step 1 Project Creation and Image Import

  • Create a new project in the chosen software.
  • Import all images for all bands. It is crucial to add directories rather than individual images to ensure all associated files and metadata are correctly linked, especially for multi-camera systems.
  • The software should automatically recognize MicaSense cameras from their metadata.
  • Ensure the correct band order is maintained:
    • RedEdge-MX: Blue, Green, Red, Red Edge, NIR
    • Altum: Blue, Green, Red, Red Edge, NIR, LWIR
    • RedEdge-MX Dual Cam: Blue-444, Blue, Green-531, Green, Red-650, Red, Red Edge-705, Red Edge, Red Edge-740, NIR

Step 2 Radiometric Calibration

This is a fundamental step to convert raw digital numbers into meaningful reflectance or radiance values.

  • The process typically uses images of the CRP captured before and after flight, and/or data from the DLS.
  • It compensates for sensor characteristics (black level, sensitivity, gain, exposure) and lens vignette effects.
  • Software-specific processes:
    • Pix4D: Options include "Camera only," "Camera and Sun Irradiance" (uses DLS), and "Camera, Sun Irradiance and Sun angle"
    • Metashape: Use "Calibrate Reflectance" which can locate panels and use DLS data
  • It's generally recommended not to perform any pre-processing on the raw imagery before importing into specialized software like Pix4Dfields or Metashape, as these tools are designed to work with the original raw data and metadata for optimal calibration.

Step 3 Photogrammetric Processing and Outputs

  • Image Alignment

    Software identifies matching features across images to determine camera positions and orientations, creating a sparse point cloud.

  • Dense Point Cloud Generation

    A detailed 3D point cloud of the surveyed area is created.

  • Digital Surface Model (DSM) / Digital Elevation Model (DEM) Generation

    A raster grid representing the elevation of the surface is created from the point cloud.

  • Orthomosaic / Reflectance Map Generation

    Individual band images are orthorectified and mosaicked to create a georeferenced map. For multispectral analysis, the primary output is a reflectance map for each band, or a stacked multi-band reflectance map. These maps contain pixel values representing surface reflectance. While an RGB orthomosaic can be generated, it is often color-balanced for visual appeal and may not be suitable for quantitative analysis. Reflectance maps preserve the radiometric accuracy.

Step 4 Vegetation Index Calculation

Once reflectance maps are generated, various vegetation indices (e.g., NDVI, NDRE, SAVI) can be calculated using raster algebra on the different spectral bands to assess vegetation health, stress, or other characteristics.

Specific Considerations for Altum Thermal Data

  • The LWIR thermal imager in Altum sensors is typically factory-radiometrically calibrated.
  • In Pix4D, when performing radiometric calibration, the correction type for the LWIR band should be set to "No Correction".
  • Thermal data can be processed to generate temperature maps, often in degrees Celsius. This may involve using raster calculator functions in GIS software or specific tools within the processing suite.

Thermal Data Best Practice

For optimal thermal data quality, allow the Altum/Altum-PT to warm up for 5-10 minutes before flight to ensure sensor thermal equilibrium. Thermal data is most useful when acquired early morning or late afternoon when thermal contrasts in vegetation are greatest. Avoid flying in high winds as they can create artificial temperature variations in the data.

Specific Considerations for Pan-Sharpening

Sensors like Altum-PT include a higher-resolution panchromatic band. This allows for pan-sharpening, a process that fuses the panchromatic band with the lower-resolution multispectral bands to create higher-resolution multispectral imagery, enhancing visual detail and the ability to discern smaller features.

Software like Agisoft Metashape and Pix4Dfields support pan-sharpening for compatible MicaSense sensors.

Merging Bands

Some processing software (like Pix4Dmapper) may output individual GeoTIFF files for each spectral band of the reflectance map. These can be merged into a single multi-band raster file for easier analysis in GIS software.

GDAL Method

The gdalbuildvrt command-line tool can be used to create a virtual raster (VRT) stacking the individual bands, which can then be converted to a single GeoTIFF if needed.

# Create a virtual raster from multiple band files
gdalbuildvrt -separate output.vrt blue.tif green.tif red.tif rededge.tif nir.tif

# Convert virtual raster to GeoTIFF
gdal_translate output.vrt output_multiband.tif

QGIS Method

The "Build Virtual Raster" tool in QGIS can achieve a similar result:

  1. Open QGIS and add all individual band GeoTIFFs
  2. Go to Raster → Miscellaneous → Build Virtual Raster
  3. Select all band files in the correct order
  4. Check "Place each input file into a separate band"
  5. Run the tool to create a VRT
  6. To save as GeoTIFF, right-click the VRT and select "Export As..."

Key Output Products

The pipeline yields several valuable data products:

Reflectance Maps

GeoTIFF files (per band or multi-band stack) where pixel values represent surface reflectance. These are the primary data for quantitative analysis.

Digital Surface Models

Raster grids representing surface topography, useful for terrain analysis and volume calculations.

Orthomosaics

Georeferenced image mosaics. For multispectral data, these are often visualized as false-color composites (e.g., CIR) for qualitative assessment.

Vegetation Indices

Calculated from reflectance maps to highlight specific vegetation characteristics (e.g., NDVI, NDRE).

Thermal Maps

Temperature maps from Altum/Altum-PT, used for applications like water stress detection or irrigation management.

Point Clouds

3D representations of the surveyed area, useful for detailed structural analysis and visualization.

Conclusion

The MicaSense RedEdge-MX Dual Camera system and the Altum/Altum-PT sensors provide powerful capabilities for acquiring detailed multispectral and thermal imagery. A successful operational pipeline, encompassing meticulous sensor selection and understanding, careful drone integration, rigorous pre-flight planning including radiometric calibration protocols, best practices in data acquisition, and appropriate data processing techniques, is essential to generate accurate and reliable data products.

By following the outlined steps and utilizing suitable processing software, users can unlock the full potential of these sensors for a wide range of remote sensing applications. While the RedEdge-MX Dual Camera and original Altum are legacy systems, the principles of their operation and data handling remain relevant for understanding multispectral data acquisition and processing in general.
